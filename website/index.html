If you track your baby’s sleep, you can contribute to a community-driven effort to make baby sleep data available to all parents. By sharing your data, you help build an open tool and support research that benefits families everywhere.

Why Baby Sleep is Still a Mystery

When I had my daughter Luna, I became obsessed with baby sleep.

Like many parents, I read books, scrolled through Reddit threads, and tried to make sense of all the conflicting advice.

I just wanted answers: Is my baby sleeping enough? Too much? Are all these night wakes normal?

One day, when Luna was six months old, I looked up how much daytime sleep she should be getting.

Here’s what I found:

  • Huckleberry: 2.5–3.5 hours (source)
  • Napper: 4 hours (source)

Both seemed reasonable. But why the difference?

I thought there must be a large study showing how much babies actually sleep at different ages—just like the charts doctors use for height and weight. But after searching, I found nothing. No real numbers, just expert opinions and small studies.

And I wasn’t alone. Every parenting forum I visited was full of parents asking the same questions:

"My baby is five months old and only naps two hours a day—is that normal?"

"How much should a seven-month-old sleep at night?"

"My nine-month-old wakes up four times a night—is anyone else dealing with this?"

That’s when it hit me: Millions of parents track their baby’s sleep. Why isn’t this data just shown somewhere?

Don’t Sleep Apps Already Have This Data?

Yes, but they don’t share it with us.

Apps like Huckleberry and Napper collect large amounts of baby sleep data, but parents don’t get to see:

  • Real distributions of baby sleep across different ages
  • Comparisons to other babies in a similar stage

We can do better. What if this data wasn’t just used behind the scenes, but was open and available to help all parents?

What We Can Build Together

If you’re here, you’re probably part of an online community of parents who swap advice, share experiences, and try to figure out baby sleep together.

What if we pooled our data to build something useful for everyone?

This is exactly what OpenSleep is about:

  • An open dataset that parents, researchers, and doctors can access
  • A free online tool where you can compare your baby’s sleep to a large sample of real babies

When we reach 1,000 contributors, I’ll compile and share a de-deintifed dataset of baby sleep and an online tool for everyone to use.

How I Handle Privacy

I know that sharing data is a big decision, and I want to be completely transparent about how this works.

  • The data I collect comes only from tracking apps. I don’t collect personal details like names or emails.
  • Everything follows GDPR standards.
  • Before re-sharing the data openely, I will harmonize the data and apply additional privacy protections. This includes removing precise timestamps to make sure the dataset remains privacy-preserving while still useful for research.
  • Your data will only not be used for commercial purposes.

You remain in control. You’ll receive a clear terms and conditions document outlining everything.

If you have questions, reach out and ask.

Who Am I

I’m Laura Alessandretti, an Associate Professor in Modelling Human Behaviour at the Computer Science department of the Technical University of Denmark.

My research focuses on large-scale digital data—I’ve studied how people move, interact online, and make decisions. My work has been published in journals like Nature, Science Advances, and Nature Human Behaviour.
